The world’s oldest skyscrapers?

25 May 2017Spain

Built in the Middle Ages, Cuenca’s skyscrapers sit precariously on the edge of a cliff, offering residents spectacular views of the Spanish countryside.

City on a hill

Perched on steep mountain slopes overlooking the gorges of the Huécar and Júcar rivers, around 170km east of Madrid, is Spain’s medieval city of Cuenca. The city blends into the natural landscape and towers over the Spanish countryside made famous in Miguel de Cervantes’ novel, Don Quixote.

A coveted fortress

A surprisingly well-preserved example of a medieval fortified city, Cuenca was originally built by the Moors in the 8th Century, and had both Muslim and Christian rulers. It was conquered by Alfonso VIII of Castile in the 12th Century, and was partially destroyed in the Peninsular War (part of the Napoleonic Wars) in the early 1800s.

Although only a tower, two stone blocks and an arch remain of its fortified walls, many important buildings have survived, including Spain's first Gothic cathedral, the Basílica de Nuestra Señora de Gracia, and El Castillo, the remains of its Arab fortress. Given the significance of its architecture, Unesco declared Cuenca a World Heritage Site in 1996.

Medieval skyscrapers

The fortifications meant that there was little room to expand the city, so Cuenca residents went up, constructing slim, tall buildings all around the San Martín neighbourhood to cater to an increasing population.

It remains unclear when these ‘skyscrapers’ were built, but experts date them to the late Middle Ages ‒ sometime between the 11th and 15th Centuries. This would make Cuenca’s skyscrapers among the oldest in the world.

Buildings of many angles

Architects adapted to the undulating landscape by building into the cliffs themselves, which resulted in buildings that appear to be different heights depending on the viewer’s perspective.

From Alfonso VIII Street (pictured), the buildings are between three and five floors tall. However, to someone standing on adjacent Santa Catalina Street – which is at a lower elevation than Alfonso VIII Street – the same buildings stand between 10 and 12 storeys high.

Hanging Houses

Other fascinating examples of Cuenca’s architecture are the famous Casas Colgadas, or Hanging Houses, which feature wooden balconies jutting out over the Huécar River. The Hanging Houses have not undergone major restoration like many others in the city, and still retain all their original elements. Three of the hanging houses can be visited; inside one of them is the Museum of Spanish Abstract Art.

Ancient homes

Some of Cuenca’s 55,000 residents still live in these skyscrapers, although many of the buildings have gone through remodelling and restoration in the last century. For residents, the lack of lifts is made up for with sweeping views over the historic town and verdant countryside.

An overwhelming presence

Walking Cuenca’s narrow streets is a dramatic experience. “The buildings are impressive from a distance, but when you stand right below them and look up, their presence is overwhelming,” photographer Rodolfo Contreras said. “With 10 floors between you and the sky, one can only imagine the architectural prowess it took to build these more than 500 years ago.”
